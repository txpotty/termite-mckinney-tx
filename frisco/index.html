Subterranean Termite Treatments

Subterranean termites are the most common type of termite found in the Frisco area. Our treatment for subterranean termites typically involves creating a protective barrier around the foundation of your home. This barrier can be established using liquid termiticides applied to the soil, effectively preventing termites from entering your home from the ground. We carefully assess the soil conditions, foundation type, and the extent of the infestation to determine the optimal placement and type of barrier. In some cases, we may also recommend the use of termite baiting systems, which are highly effective at eliminating entire colonies by luring termites to bait stations where they consume a slow-acting toxicant that they then carry back to the colony. This method can be particularly useful in situations where traditional liquid treatments are challenging to apply or less desirable. The goal of subterranean termite treatment is to eliminate the existing infestation and prevent future access to your home's structure. We understand that these treatments require precision and adherence to strict safety protocols, and our technicians are thoroughly trained in the proper application of all products used. Protecting the structural integrity of your home from the insatiable appetites of subterranean termites is paramount, and our treatments are designed for long-term effectiveness.

Drywood Termite Treatments

Drywood termites are less common than subterranean termites but can still cause significant damage by directly infesting wooden structural components and furniture without requiring contact with the soil. Treating a drywood termite infestation requires a different approach. Our treatments for drywood termites may include localized treatments, such as injecting termiticides directly into infested wood, or in more extensive infestations, fumigation may be necessary. Fumigation involves covering the entire structure and introducing a gas that penetrates all wood within the building, effectively killing all termites present. This method is highly effective for widespread infestations but requires temporary vacancy of the property. We carefully evaluate the scope of the drywood termite infestation to determine the most appropriate treatment method, always prioritizing the most effective and least disruptive solution for your specific situation. Our technicians are experienced in identifying the subtle signs of drywood termites, such as fecal pellets (frass) and tiny kick-out holes in wood, and are skilled in applying targeted treatments to eliminate these pests and prevent further damage. Protecting valuable wooden elements in your home from the destructive potential of drywood termites is a key aspect of our service.

Common Termite Problems and How We Can Help

Recognizing the signs of a termite infestation early is crucial in minimizing damage and reducing the cost of repairs. Many homeowners may not realize they have a termite problem until the damage is significant. Understanding the common indicators can help you identify a potential issue and seek professional help promptly. At McKinney Termite, we are experts in identifying and addressing all types of termite-related issues in Frisco, TX.

Mud Tubes on Foundation or Walls

One of the most common and visible signs of subterranean termites is the presence of mud tubes. These pencil-width tunnels are constructed by termites using soil, wood particles, and their saliva, serving as protective pathways between their colony in the soil and their food source (your home's wooden structure). Finding these tubes on your foundation, walls, or in crawl spaces indicates active termite foraging. Disturbing or breaking a mud tube and seeing live termites inside is an undeniable sign of infestation. These tubes are essential for subterranean termites as they require high humidity and protection from predators and dry air to travel between their colony underground and the wood they consume in your home. The appearance of even a few small mud tubes warrants immediate professional inspection, as this is often just the tip of the iceberg of a larger subterranean termite colony at work. Our team specializes in locating these mud tubes, tracing them back to their origin, and implementing targeted treatments to disrupt these pathways and eliminate the colony. Ignoring mud tubes can lead to significant, widespread structural damage over time, as the termites continue to access and consume the wood within your home. Early detection through recognizing mud tubes is vital for prompt action.

Wings Discarded Near Windows or Doors

Termite swarming is a natural process where winged reproductive termites (swarmers) leave the colony to establish new ones. These swarmers are often attracted to light and may emerge from cracks or openings in your home. After swarming, they shed their wings, often leaving small piles of translucent wings near windowsills, doors, or other light sources. The presence of discarded wings is a strong indicator of a nearby termite colony, even if you don't see the termites themselves. Termite swarming in Frisco typically occurs during warmer months, often after a rain. While the swarmers themselves are not destructive, their presence signifies that a mature, active colony is located nearby, potentially within your home's structure or on your property. Finding these discarded wings should be a red flag prompting a professional termite inspection. Our experts can differentiate between termite wings and ant wings, which can look similar, and use the presence of discarded wings as a key piece of evidence to uncover the full extent of an infestation. Ignoring discarded wings means allowing a potential termite infestation to continue unchecked, potentially leading to extensive and costly damage to your home's wooden structure over time. Acting quickly based on this visual cue is a smart move for any homeowner.

Damaged or Hollow-Sounding Wood

Termites consume cellulose, which is the main component of wood. As they tunnel through wooden structures, they create hollowed-out areas within beams, walls, or furniture. Tapping on infested wood may produce a hollow or dull sound, indicating that the interior has been compromised. Visibly damaged wood, such as buckling floors, sagging ceilings, or warped walls, can also be a sign of advanced termite infestation. If you notice any areas of your home where the wood appears soft, weakened, or shows signs of tunneling (galleries), it is highly probable that termites are actively feeding. The internal damage caused by termites is often hidden and can be extensive before becoming outwardly apparent. Termite activity can weaken the structural integrity of your home, potentially leading to significant safety hazards and costly repairs. Our thorough inspections involve probing and sounding techniques to identify areas of concealed termite damage. We can assess the extent of the damage and recommend the necessary repairs in addition to eliminating the active infestation. Discovering damaged or hollow-sounding wood means that termites have been at work for some time, and immediate intervention is required to prevent further deterioration and potential structural failure. Don't delay in seeking professional evaluation if you suspect damaged wood in your home.

Bubbling or Peeling Paint (Especially Near Wood)

While bubbling or peeling paint can be caused by various factors, including moisture issues, it can also be a sign of termites. Termites introduce moisture into the wood as they consume it, and this excess moisture can cause the paint on the surface to blister, bubble, or peel. This is often seen on wooden door frames, window sills, and baseboards. If you notice paint irregularities, particularly in areas where wood is present, it's worth investigating further. The moisture introduced by termites can be a distinct and early sign of their presence, even before visible wood damage becomes apparent. This is particularly true for subterranean termites, which require high humidity levels to thrive. Bubbling or peeling paint that seems unrelated to typical water leaks or humidity changes should raise suspicion. Our experienced technicians understand the subtle signs of termite activity and can differentiate between termite-related paint issues and those caused by other factors. Ignoring this seemingly minor paint problem could allow a termite infestation to escalate unnoticed, leading to more significant damage. A professional inspection can determine if the paint issue is a symptom of a hidden termite problem requiring prompt action. Pay attention to these less obvious indicators of potential termite activity.

Fecal Pellets (Frass) - For Drywood Termites

Drywood termites, unlike subterranean termites, do not build mud tubes. Instead, they create small kick-out holes in the wood and expel their fecal pellets, also known as frass, from these openings. Termite frass are tiny, hard, six-sided pellets that often resemble sawdust or sand. Finding small piles of these pellets, particularly near wooden furniture, structural beams, or along baseboards, is a definitive sign of a drywood termite infestation. The color of the frass can vary depending on the type of wood the termites are consuming. Drywood termite frass is a direct byproduct of their feeding activity and a clear indicator of their presence within the wood. The consistent appearance of these tiny pellets in specific locations should prompt immediate investigation by a pest control professional. Drywood termite infestations can be difficult to detect externally until the damage is significant, making the presence of fras one of the most reliable indicators. Our team is experienced in identifying drywood termite frass and locating the kick-out holes from which they are expelled, allowing us to pinpoint the extent of the infestation and implement targeted treatments. Ignoring the presence of what might seem like harmless sawdust could allow a drywood termite colony to continue damaging your valuable wooden property. If you encounter these distinct fecal pellets, it's time to call in the experts.
